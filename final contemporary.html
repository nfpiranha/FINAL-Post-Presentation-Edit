CONTEMPORARY

The works presented here represent the newer acclaimed works, usually pertaining to the 20th Century and beyond. These forms encompass a wide variety of genres and styles that were so startling and groundbreaking when they first debuted that art was never the same, and many questions were raised on whether or no it even was art, and therefore what art was supposed to be. For all its questions and aesthetics, contemporary art thrives in museums around the world.

OOF (1963) by Edward Rusca is a strange piece of pop-art that managed to blend visuals with orality and comic books. The art is styled based off of the actions and expressions of comic strips and action, but one can't help but uttering the word "OOF' aloud or at leas tthinking it upon viewing, begging the quesion if it really is a word, or if this is really even art.

Pipilotti Rist: Pixel Forest, debuted in the New Museum of Contemporary Art in 2016, and is a combination of strange, illuminous installations and videos from Rist's work in New York. Strange, interactive, and hypnotic, this exhibit branches beyond the canvas to bring people in and immerse them.

Circular Forms (1930) by Robert Dulaunay is an array of color and geometry, combining math and artistic expression in an abstract, yet controlled way. The art here is both aestheticall and geometrically pleasing as well as instantly eye-catching because of its lush colors and composition. Despite being relatively early, it was a bold move for later abstract and minimalist work.

LifeStyle Wars by Anika Yi is a display that combines strange sci-fi architecture and real, living ants to create a futuristic, yet organic and advanced modern piece that stuns the viewer in person.
